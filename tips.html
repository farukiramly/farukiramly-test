Cooking Tips & Tricks

Shut the stove off before
eggs are done

The trick to great eggs is never leave a surface in contact too long with the skillet to not overcook them! Whether you scramble, fry or poach, always turn off the stove a few minutes before ... the eggs look done even when they seem a bit runny or when the eggs are 90% cooked. The remainder of the heat will cook them to fluffy perfection. This technique may take a bit of practice, but it is quite simple.

Use pasta water to create a pasta sauce

If you thought your pasta tasted good before, wait until you try this trick. The salty water will add more flavor to your dish. Reserved pasta water also contain starch that can be used to adjust the consistency of sauce... Pasta water works best when added to sauce made in a pan—that means noodles enjoyed with marinara sauce or Bolognese don't really have a need for the starchy cooking water. But should you find that your sauce is too thick, or that it isn't clinging to your pasta enough, instead of adding more oil use a little pasta water to thin it out. Knowing when to use pasta water just takes a bit of training. Think a step ahead: Catch yourself before you drain the pasta in a colander, and ladle a cup or two of water from the pot. Even more efficient is the professional cook's technique: Skip the colander and scoop the cooked pasta with a mesh spider directly from the pot into a waiting skillet. To set up for this restaurant-like method, follow these simple steps: While your pasta water is coming to a boil, create the base of your sauce in a large skillet. Melt butter or heat olive oil, and cook onions, garlic, or other soffrito. This is also the point at which you would cook sausage or cured meats, such as pancetta, guanciale, or bacon, rendering the fat and browning the meat. Next you'll build another layer of flavor: Add any vegetables that you're including. Give them space in the pan if they need it. Mushrooms don't like to be crowded and should be cooked in batches, whereas dark, leafy greens can be piled on top of each other and tossed as they cook and wilt down into a smaller amount.

Chop herbs with salt

To prevent herbs from flying all over the place when chopping, sprinkle a bit of salt onto the board. The coarse salt gives the whole chopping process some "teeth" and this will keep them in the place. If you’ve got some leftover ... once you’re done chopping, our next tip will keep them from going to waste. The salt also dries out the herbs preserving their vivid flavor for months. You can improvise endlessly on the basic formula by improvising your own mix of herb. Thyme, rosemary, and savory with a touch of lavender will make a lovely Provencal herb salt. The herb salt makes a great gift. If you want to make a lot of herb salt, Do it in batches, rather than all at once, to retain the right texture.
